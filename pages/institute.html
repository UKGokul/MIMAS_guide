This project is connected to the Leibniz Institute of Atmospheric Physics (IAP), Kühlungsborn — with a focus on the Department of Optical Soundings and Sounding Rockets.

Leibniz Institute of Atmospheric Physics (IAP)

The IAP investigates processes in the middle and upper atmosphere, with particular emphasis on the stratosphere, mesosphere, and lower thermosphere (MLT). The MLT region forms a critical link between the troposphere (the “weather” layer) and near-space environments relevant for low-Earth-orbiting satellites.

Department: Optical Soundings & Sounding Rockets

The main objectives of the Department of Optical Soundings and Sounding Rockets are ground-based optical soundings of the middle and upper atmosphere (and, in part, the troposphere), and the scientific interpretation of processes in these regions. The core experimental method is lidar (LIght Detection And Ranging), enabling active, long-term, ground-based observations of atmospheric parameters and constituents across approximately 0–120 km altitude.

Complementary measurements are obtained using in-situ detectors on stratospheric balloons and sounding rockets. A further key goal is the modelling and theoretical interpretation of the processes observed experimentally — where models like MIMAS play an important role.

Department Director: Dr. Gerd Baumgarten

Research Focus Areas

  • Exploration of the middle atmosphere (MLT): Remote sensing is essential in this altitude range; laser-based lidar systems provide key observations alongside occasional rocket in-situ measurements.
  • Temperature and wind soundings: Measurements across mid-latitudes, the Arctic, and Antarctica to quantify variability and long-term change.
  • Noctilucent clouds (NLC): NLCs serve as a sensitive tracer of temperatures in the ~80–90 km region and reveal fine-scale dynamics.
  • Metal layers and trace gases: Studies of chemistry and dynamics in the MLT using observations of metallic layers and other species.

Vertical Coupling

Atmospheric layers are dynamically coupled by waves propagating horizontally and vertically. As a result, changes in the troposphere and stratosphere can influence the mesosphere and thermosphere — and vice versa. Understanding the middle and upper atmosphere therefore also supports improved understanding of weather, climate variability, and long-term change.

  • Tides: signatures in temperature, NLC occurrence, and metal layers
  • Planetary waves: winter-time impacts on circulation and composition
  • Gravity waves: effects in wind, temperature, and trace-gas distributions
  • Turbulence: including turbulence in the stratosphere and implications for transport

Long-Term Change & NLC Trends

Climate change affects not only the lower atmosphere but also the middle atmosphere. In the mesosphere, increasing carbon dioxide can lead to a cooling effect. The department aims to understand the magnitude of these changes, regional differences, and their influence on dynamics and trace-gas distributions.

  • Temperature trends from observations and modelling
  • Long-term evolution and variability of mesospheric ice and NLC occurrence
